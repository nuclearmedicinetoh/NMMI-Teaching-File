Diagnosis:

  • Degenerative knee disease with synovitis due to prior ACL repair and lateral meniscal tear repair.

Summarized Report:

  • Flow study of 99mTc-MDP bone scan shows mild hyperperfusion overlying the left knee.
  • Blood pool phase imaging also demonstrates mild hyperemia in the left knee, projecting on the lateral aspect. The uptake also conforms to the femoral condyle in the lateral view as well as in the posterior aspect of the left knee joint.
  • The delayed static imaging shows an intense uptake in the left knee overlying the lateral aspect as well as activity in the joint space similar to blood pool imaging.
  • The SPECT-CT imaging shows the radiotracer activity in the lateral joint compartment at the location of the lateral meniscus. Indeed, uptake is seen overlying prior ACL repair along side the path of the ligament and at the tibial insertion.
  • There is low grade uptake in the medial compartment with a mild joint effusion. MRI at the time of bone scan demonstrated status post ACL reconstruction. Focal mild to moderate thinning of the mid part of the ACL graft which appears heterogenous.
  • Markedly diminutive lateral meniscus due to prior partial meniscectomy with interval development of complete radial tear of the posterior horn, which was markedly attenuated and a horizontal tear of the anterior horn of the lateral meniscus. MRI also showed focal areas of high-grade tricompartmental chondropathy, as detailed above.

Teaching Point:

  • Ligaments in the knee primarily resist tensile forces to maintain joint stability, with the cruciate ligaments playing a critical role in controlling tibial translation during flexion.
  • The anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) provides more than 80% of the resistance to anteroposterior tibial translation between 30¬∞ and 90¬∞ of knee flexion.
  • At higher flexion angles, the medial collateral ligament (MCL) becomes increasingly important in stabilizing the joint. Additionally, the ACL helps prevent internal tibial rotation; without it, the tibia undergoes increased rotational movement, leading to instability.
  • In an ACL-deficient knee, this loss of primary stability results in excessive anterior tibial translation and heightened rotational stress, which contributes to joint instability. To compensate for the ACL‚Äôs absence, secondary structures such as the menisci become more involved in resisting forces, which makes them more vulnerable to damage and degeneration.
  • ACL injury, particularly when accompanied by meniscal or other ligamentous damage, significantly increases the risk of developing osteoarthritis. Even after ACL reconstruction, the risk of osteoarthritis remains elevated for many patients.
  • Synovial fluid biomarkers in both ACL-deficient and reconstructed knees show increased collagen turnover. Moreover, reconstructed knees exhibit continued elevation of synovial inflammatory cytokines postoperatively, compared to preoperative levels. These inflammatory markers contribute to synovitis and exacerbate cartilage damage, potentially accelerating joint degeneration.
  • The patient finally underwent total knee replacement. The surgical note highlighted an extensive synovitis component.
