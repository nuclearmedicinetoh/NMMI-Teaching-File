Gastrointestinal Studies – Case #1

Patient Information

  • 69-year-old male, colonoscopy demonstrated blood in the colon suspicious for a small bowel source of bleeding.

Key Findings:

Diagnosis:

  • Small bowel hemorrhage.

Summarized Report:

  • Dynamic images demonstrate a focal extravasation of the radioactive tracer in the RLQ with moving forward in small bowel.
  • The SPECT-CT imaging right after dynamic imaging demonstrates radioactivity in the small bowel.

Teaching Point:

  • Lower gastrointestinal bleeding can occur due to underlying causes differ between age groups. In younger patients, the most frequent causes are inflammatory bowel disease, polyps, Meckel diverticulum, and clotting disorders however, in older adults, common causes include colonic diverticulosis (~ 40%), angiodysplasia, colorectal cancer, and polyps.
  • Other conditions like fissures, hemorrhoid, and also sometimes in patients with upper gastrointestinal bleeding from conditions like peptic ulcers and the effects of anticoagulant therapy can also contribute to lower GI bleeding.
  • Various modalities are available for lower GI bleed localization. CT Angiography (CTA) is a very sensitive modality for active, fast, arterial bleeding, and can detect small bleeds down to >0.35 mL/min.
  • Interventional angiography is the gold standard for detecting active bleeding, especially small, intermittent, or slow bleeding in the rate more than 0.5-1 ml/min. This modality is also the best for real-time intervention.
  • Tc99m RBC Scan has a moderate sensitivity and particularly is useful for chronic or slow, intermittent bleeds (>0.1 mL/min). It may miss rapid, active arterial bleeding. Regardless of the bleeding rate, 2–3 ml of pooled, extravasated blood is necessary for detection by a gamma camera.
  • Tc99m Sulfur Colloid (SC) scan is the most sensitive modality for very low rate of bleeding at 0.05-0.1 ml/min.
  • For acute and high flow bleeding interventional and CT angiography are preferred, however for chronic and slow rate of bleeding An RBC scan is preferable if no evidence of bleeding is visualized on CT angiography.
  • The overall sensitivity and specificity of the RBC scan in detecting lower GI bleeding have been reported as 84.6% (22/26) and 70.4% (38/54), respectively, with the accuracy of localization of bleeding sites in patients with confirmed positive scans being 72.7%.
