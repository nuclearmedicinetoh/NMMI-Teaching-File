Bone Studies – Case #2

Patient Information

  • 28-year-old male with left calf mass, referred for further assessment with bone scan.

Key Findings:

Diagnosis:

  • Parosteal osteosarcoma.

Summarized Report:

  • A triphasic bone scan using 99mTc-MDP show triple positive increased uptake in perfusion, blood pool and delayed imaging in the superomedial of the left calf.
  • Corresponding on an exophytic sclerotic bony lesion adjacent to the posteromedial cortex of the left proximal tibial metadiaphysis.
  • The lesion appears to originate from very superficial aspect of the underlying bone cortex.

Teaching Point:

  • Osteosarcoma includes several types of bone tumors, with the most common form being a high-grade osteosarcoma that develops within the bone marrow.
  • Some variants of the disease originate on the outer cortical surface of the bone, and these are referred to as surface osteosarcomas.
  • Surface osteosarcomas are further classified into parosteal osteosarcoma, periosteal osteosarcoma, and high-grade surface osteosarcoma.
  • Parosteal osteosarcoma is a subtype of osteosarcoma, a low grade, well differentiated fibroblastic, slow growing tumor that produces bone/osteoid matrix, arising from the surface of the bone at the outer layer of the periosteum, like cauliflower-shaped, attaching to outer fibrotic cortex of the periosteum in metadiaphysis around the knee and proximal humerus with a narrow zone which appears as a relatively hypodensity zone on CT scan or X-Ray (string sign).
  • This pattern is different from periosteal osteosarcoma which radiographically, presents as a partially mineralized, broad-based lesion located on the diaphysis which attaches to a thickened periosteum, with aggressive periosteal features and external erosion of the underlying bone cortex, often creating a crater-like depression on the surface.
  • Bone scan is a main diagnostic tool which shows uptake in the site of lesion and its metastasis.
